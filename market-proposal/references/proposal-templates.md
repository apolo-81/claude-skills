# Proposal Templates Reference

## Pricing Psychology and Tier Construction

### Why Three Tiers Work

The three-tier structure (Good-Better-Best) works because of anchoring and contrast effects:

1. **Anchoring:** The first price seen becomes the reference point. Present the highest tier first, and the middle tier feels reasonable by contrast.
2. **Decision simplification:** Three options is the cognitive sweet spot. Two options feel like a binary choice. Four or more options cause decision paralysis.
3. **Middle-tier preference:** When given three options, most people choose the middle — it feels like the "smart" choice (not too cheap, not overspending).
4. **Upsell pathway:** Even if the client signs at the lowest tier, the structure signals there's more available when they're ready to grow.

**Naming conventions that work:**
- Growth / Accelerate / Dominate
- Starter / Professional / Enterprise
- Essential / Pro / Elite
- Foundation / Scale / Accelerate

Avoid: Basic / Standard / Premium (these feel generic and commodity-like)

### Tier Construction Rules

1. **Bottom tier:** Should be something you can profitably deliver and that genuinely solves the client's core problem. Not a stripped-down version that sets them up to fail.
2. **Middle tier:** The tier you want most clients on. Price it at 2-3x the bottom tier. Include the most valuable differentiators.
3. **Top tier:** Price it at 2-2.5x the middle tier. This tier should be clearly worth it for the right client — not artificially inflated.
4. **Clear differentiation:** Each tier should have 2-3 features that are meaningfully different — not just "more of the same." Volume-only differentiation (4 posts vs 8 posts) is weak. Add qualitative differences (strategy involvement, response time, dedicated team member).

- Bonus at 20% above target (common in paid ads and SEO)
- Revenue share on incremental revenue generated (1-5% common range)
- Success bonuses for hitting specific milestones (keep at most 20-30% of total comp)

## Objection Response Scripts

### "Your price is too high"

**What they're really saying:** I don't see enough value relative to the cost.

**Response:**
"I understand. Let me show you the math.

Currently you're generating [X leads/month] at [X% conversion rate]. That's [X revenue/month] from marketing.

If we improve your conversion rate by [X%] and increase traffic by [X%] — which is conservative based on similar clients — you're looking at [X additional revenue/month].

At [retainer price], that's a [X]x return in 6 months. Does that change how you're thinking about the investment?

If the budget is genuinely constrained, we can start with [specific smaller scope] at [lower price] and expand as we see results."

**Smaller scope option to have ready:**
- Start with one high-impact service area (usually SEO or paid ads)
- 90-day pilot with specific success criteria before a longer commitment
- Monthly instead of 3-month minimum commitment (with a higher monthly rate)

---

### "We can do this in-house"

**What they're really saying:** I'm questioning whether your expertise justifies the cost vs. a hire.

**Response:**
"That's a completely valid option. Here's how the numbers typically compare:

An in-house marketing hire at [seniority level] costs $[salary range] in salary alone — plus benefits, equipment, management time, and the reality that one person rarely has the full skill set you need.

With us, you get a full team: strategy, copy, design, analytics, ads management, and SEO specialists — all with results across [X] clients.

The other factor is time. A new hire takes 3-6 months to ramp up and prove their approach. We can have campaigns live within [X] weeks with methodologies we've already refined.

If you're committed to building in-house eventually, we can help you set up the infrastructure now and document our playbook, so when you do hire, they're inheriting a working system rather than starting from scratch."

---

### "We tried an agency before and it didn't work"

**What they're really saying:** I've been burned before. I need to understand why you're different.

**Response:**
"That's important context — can you tell me what specifically didn't work? Was it the results, the communication, the fit, or something else?"

[Listen carefully. Then:]

"Based on what you've described, here's what I'd do differently:

[Specific to what they shared — e.g., "You said they went dark for 3 weeks at a time — we send a weekly update every Friday whether you ask for it or not." / "You said they ran the same campaigns for months without iteration — we review performance weekly and pivot within 48 hours of seeing something isn't working."]

I'd also suggest starting with a 90-day pilot with specific success criteria defined upfront, so we both have clear accountability. If we don't hit [specific metric], you have clear grounds to walk away."

---

### "We need to think about it" / "We'll get back to you"

**What they're really saying:** Something didn't click — they're not ready to say yes.

**Response (in the meeting):**
"Of course. What specific parts would be most helpful to think through? Is it the scope, the price, the timeline, or something else?"

[If they give a specific concern, address it now — don't leave it unresolved.]

"I'll follow up on [specific day] at [time]. Does that work? And if something comes up in the meantime, I'm always reachable at [contact]."

**Follow-up email (Day 2):**
"Hi [name], following up on our conversation. I wanted to share [one relevant case study or insight related to their specific situation]. Happy to get on a quick call to walk through any remaining questions. Available [specific times]."

---

### "Can you guarantee results?"

**What they're really saying:** I'm scared this won't work and I'll have wasted money.

**Response:**
"I understand the need for certainty. Here's what I can and can't commit to:

What I can commit to: [Specific deliverables — reporting cadence, the work that gets done, response times, the methodology.]

What I can't guarantee: Specific revenue or traffic numbers, because they depend on factors we both influence — your offer's appeal, your pricing, your sales process, market conditions.

What I can share: Our track record with similar clients. [Share 2-3 relevant case studies with specific numbers.]

What I can offer instead of a guarantee: A 90-day pilot with specific success criteria we agree on upfront. If we don't hit [metric], we either adjust scope or you have the option to exit. Does that de-risk it enough for you?"

## Executive Summary Writing Guide

The executive summary is the single most important section. Clients often read only this and the pricing page.

**Formula:**
1. Sentence 1: Name the client's situation — specifically and accurately. Not "you want to grow." "You have strong product-market fit with [specific audience] but inconsistent lead generation is creating revenue volatility."
2. Sentence 2: Name the opportunity you identified — with data if possible. "We've identified three areas where targeted improvements could generate 40-60% more qualified leads within 6 months."
3. Sentence 3: The approach you're recommending and why. "We recommend a phased approach beginning with [service], which gives the fastest path to measurable results without overhauling systems that are already working."

**What to avoid:**
- Generic opener ("We are excited to submit this proposal...")
- Listing your services before establishing understanding of their situation
- Adjectives without evidence ("our world-class team," "proven methodology")
- More than 3 paragraphs — executives skim

## ROI Calculation Guide

### Base Formula

```
Monthly Revenue Impact = (Δ Traffic × Conversion Rate) + (Traffic × Δ Conversion Rate)

Where:
Δ Traffic = additional monthly visits from proposed improvements
Δ Conversion Rate = improvement in conversion rate
Traffic = current monthly visits

Then multiply the additional conversions by:
Average Deal Value × Close Rate (for B2B)
Average Order Value (for e-commerce)
```

### Conservative Multipliers by Service

Use these multipliers to set realistic expectations:

| Service | Traffic Impact (6 months) | Conversion Impact | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| SEO (competitive niche) | +15-40% | — | Medium |
| SEO (low competition) | +40-100% | — | High |
| CRO / Landing page | — | +20-50% | High |
| Paid Ads (new) | +50-200% | — | Variable |
| Email Marketing | — | +5-15% on list | High |
| Content Marketing | +10-30% (3-6 month lag) | — | Low-Medium |

**Always apply a confidence factor:**
- High confidence (proven approach, similar client): 0.7
- Medium confidence (new channel for client): 0.5
- Low confidence (market uncertainty): 0.3

### Cost of Not Acting

Always include this calculation — it reframes the decision from "should I spend $X?" to "can I afford not to?"

```
Monthly opportunity cost = (Current gap from benchmark) × (Average deal value)

Example:
- Industry benchmark conversion rate: 3.5%
- Client current conversion rate: 1.8%
- Gap: 1.7%
- Monthly traffic: 8,000 visits
- Additional conversions at benchmark: 8,000 × 0.017 = 136
- Average deal value: $300
- Monthly opportunity cost: 136 × $300 = $40,800/month

= $489,600/year in unrealized revenue at current performance
```

This number — the annualized cost of the gap — makes the retainer price feel small by comparison.
